\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This report presents Cheebo, a multi-vendor e-commerce marketplace
developed as a final-year project at TEK-UP in partnership with
NeuralBey. The platform lets independent sellers open and customise
their own storefronts, manage product catalogues, and fulfil orders,
while customers browse across vendors from a single interface and
administrators moderate the marketplace. Its distinguishing feature is
the integration of Artificial Intelligence for operational tasks:
automated product and store verification, a natural-language database
assistant for administrators, and AI-driven storefront redesign, all
orchestrated as n8n workflows behind webhooks. The system is built as
a TypeScript monorepo --- a NestJS REST API backed by Prisma and
MariaDB, and a React single-page application --- and is engineered to
professional standards, with an automated test suite of 192 tests and
a complete CI/CD pipeline that containerises the services with Docker,
provisions infrastructure with Terraform, and deploys to a live
DigitalOcean environment on every push. The result is a deployed,
tested, and reproducible marketplace that demonstrates both full-stack
application development and modern DevOps and AI-integration practice.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} marketplace, multi-vendor, NestJS, React, Prisma,
  Artificial Intelligence, Docker, CI/CD, Terraform.

\section{Project Context}

\subsection{Project Origin and Motivation}

The Cheebo project originated from an internal need identified at
NeuralBey: the Tunisian online retail landscape lacks a modern,
multi-vendor platform that is both seller-friendly and equipped with
the automation features expected of contemporary marketplaces.
Existing local solutions are typically single-vendor stores or
classified-ad sites, and they offer limited tooling for moderation,
content quality and discovery.

NeuralBey saw an opportunity for a product combining three
differentiating capabilities:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item a true multi-vendor model with self-service onboarding for
        sellers;
  \item AI-assisted moderation of stores and products to reduce
        manual review load;
  \item a deployment story that demonstrates contemporary DevOps
        practices end-to-end.
\end{enumerate}

The PFE topic was therefore scoped jointly with the professional
supervisor, with the explicit goal of producing a system that could
serve both as a portfolio piece for the company and as a foundation
for further internal development beyond the internship.

\subsection{Multi-Vendor Marketplace Concept}

A \emph{multi-vendor marketplace} is a platform on which independent
sellers operate their own stores within a shared environment governed
by a central operator. It differs from two adjacent models:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item a \textbf{single-vendor store}, where the platform itself owns
        the inventory and the brand;
  \item a \textbf{classified-ad site}, where the platform only lists
        offers and plays no role in transactions or quality control.
\end{itemize}

A marketplace must reconcile three concerns simultaneously:
\textbf{seller autonomy} (each seller manages catalogue, branding and
inventory), \textbf{buyer trust} (the platform guarantees a baseline
of product authenticity and fulfilment) and \textbf{operator
governance} (administrators onboard sellers, moderate content, suspend
stores and curate featured selections). These three perspectives drive
the architecture of Cheebo: every feature is designed to balance
autonomy for sellers, trust for buyers, and control for administrators.
The integration of AI verification is a direct response to the
buyer-trust requirement at the scale a marketplace demands.

\subsection{Target Users}

Three actor categories were identified at the scoping stage:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Customer} --- an end-user who browses the catalogue,
        places orders and tracks deliveries. Expects a fast,
        mobile-friendly experience with reliable search and clear
        product information.
  \item \textbf{Seller} --- a small or medium merchant who creates a
        store, manages a product catalogue and fulfils orders.
        Expects low onboarding friction, clear analytics, and
        protection against unfair competition from low-quality
        listings.
  \item \textbf{Administrator} --- the platform operator (NeuralBey
        staff) who reviews seller applications, moderates flagged
        content, curates featured stores, and intervenes when a store
        violates the marketplace rules.
\end{itemize}

\section{Study of Existing Solutions}

\subsection{Existing Tunisian E-Commerce Platforms}

Three categories of competing platforms were studied:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Pan-African marketplaces (Jumia).} Jumia Tunisia is
        the most visible regional marketplace. It supports multiple
        vendors at scale and offers a polished customer experience,
        but seller onboarding is heavyweight and the platform is not
        tailored to small Tunisian merchants.
  \item \textbf{Classified-ad sites (Tayara, Affariyet).} Tayara
        dominates the local classified-ad market. It offers excellent
        discovery for sellers, but performs no escrow, no order
        management, no integrated payment and no quality moderation.
  \item \textbf{Single-vendor stores (MyTek, Wiki, etc.).} These are
        vertically integrated retailers that operate their own
        catalogue. They cannot be used by independent sellers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations of Current Solutions}

From the study above, four gaps emerged:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{L\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{No AI-assisted moderation.} On existing platforms,
        store and product verification are entirely manual --- slow,
        inconsistent, and impractical at scale.
  \item \textbf{Heavyweight seller onboarding.} Pan-regional players
        require contractual paperwork unsuitable for small merchants.
  \item \textbf{Limited operator tooling.} Classified-ad sites have
        weak admin dashboards; single-vendor stores have none for
        external sellers.
  \item \textbf{Outdated technical foundations.} Several platforms
        still rely on monolithic stacks without modern CI/CD or
        infrastructure-as-code, making evolution costly.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Proposed Solution}

\subsection{Cheebo Overview}

Cheebo is a multi-vendor marketplace designed around three pillars:
a self-service seller experience, AI-assisted content verification,
and a modern, fully containerised deployment. The platform is built
as a single-page \textbf{React 19} application backed by a modular
\textbf{NestJS} API, with \textbf{MariaDB} as the relational store
and \textbf{n8n} acting as an orchestration layer for AI tasks.
Authentication is handled by \textbf{Better-Auth} with email
verification, and \textbf{Prisma} is used as the ORM. The full system
is packaged with Docker Compose, provisioned on DigitalOcean via
Terraform, and delivered through a GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline.

Sellers create a store in minutes, populate it with products and
tags, and benefit from automatic AI verification that surfaces a
trust badge to potential buyers. Administrators review borderline
cases through a dedicated dashboard, suspend stores when needed, and
override automated decisions, and query the database through an
in-app AI chat assistant for operational insights. Customers browse
a unified catalogue and place orders across multiple sellers in a
single checkout.

\subsection{Key Differentiators}

Seven differentiators set Cheebo apart from the platforms surveyed in
the previous section:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{D\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{AI verification of stores and products} via an n8n
        workflow that calls a hosted LLM and writes back the
        \texttt{aiReviewed} flag plus a textual \texttt{aiReviewNote}
        rationale.
  \item \textbf{Lightweight seller onboarding} through a seller
        application form reviewed by administrators, with a free tier
        offering a single store at zero cost.
  \item \textbf{Integrated administration tooling} (review, suspend,
        curate, cascade-delete) implemented as first-class features
        rather than database admin scripts.
  \item \textbf{Conversational data access} via a database-aware
        chatbot built on n8n, allowing administrators to query the
        platform in natural language.
  \item \textbf{AI-assisted storefront redesign} --- sellers can
        regenerate their entire store visual identity from a
        natural-language prompt, powered by an n8n workflow and the
        Groq \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} model.
  \item \textbf{Store-wide sale promotions} that propagate a
        percentage discount automatically across all product cards,
        store listings, and search results, and apply the discounted
        price at checkout without any per-product editing.
  \item \textbf{Modern DevOps foundations} --- pnpm monorepo, Docker
        Compose, Terraform on DigitalOcean, and GitHub Actions
        CI/CD --- demonstrating production-grade engineering
        practices end-to-end.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Project Scope}

The scope was agreed with the professional supervisor and is split
between in-scope and out-of-scope items so that the deliverables
remain clear and bounded.

\noindent\textbf{In scope:} account creation and email verification;
seller application workflow with admin review; store and product
CRUD with image upload; tag-based product recommendations with a
top-seller fallback; order placement, items and history; admin
moderation panels (store suspension, cascade-delete); AI verification and chat assistant via n8n;
store-wide percentage sales with automatic badge propagation across products and search results;
AI-assisted storefront redesign via an n8n/Groq workflow;
premium tier unlocking additional stores; and infrastructure-as-code
deployment.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Out of scope} (deferred to future iterations):
integrated payment processing (e.g.\ Konnect or Stripe); a native
mobile application; advanced fraud detection; on-premise LLM
hosting; multi-currency support; and a full-text search engine such
as Elasticsearch or Typesense.

\section{Work Methodology}

\subsection{Classical vs Agile Approaches}

Every software project requires a methodological framework to organise
work, manage priorities, and guarantee the delivery of a quality
product. Two major families of methods have historically competed: the
\textbf{classical} (predictive) approach and the \textbf{agile}
(adaptive) approach. Their fundamental difference lies in how they
handle change and value delivery.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Classical approach --- Waterfall.}
The Waterfall method divides the project into strictly sequential
phases: requirements, design, development, testing, and delivery.
Each phase must be fully validated before the next begins. This model
suits projects whose requirements are stable and known in advance, but
it shows its limits as soon as the context evolves:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Requirements are frozen at launch; any late change is costly.
  \item The client sees a working product only at the very end of the project.
  \item Defects and risks are detected in the test phase, too late to
        address without significant impact.
  \item Documentation tends to be voluminous and quickly out of sync
        with the actual code.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Agile approach --- Core principles.}
Agility was born from the \textit{Agile Manifesto}
(2001)~\cite{agilemanifesto}, which
champions collaboration, adaptability, and the continuous delivery of
value. Rather than planning the entire project upfront, work is
organised into \textbf{short cycles called sprints} (one to four
weeks), each producing a functional and tested increment. This
approach offers several concrete advantages:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Requirements are refined continuously from client and
        stakeholder feedback.
  \item Value is delivered from the first sprints; the product is
        usable well before the end of the project.
  \item Risks are identified and addressed early.
  \item Daily communication fosters responsiveness and team alignment.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why Scrum?}

Among the agile methodologies available --- Scrum, Kanban, XP,
SAFe --- \textbf{Scrum} was chosen to manage this project for the
following reasons:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Flexibility:} the requirements of Cheebo evolved
        throughout the internship; Scrum allows supervisor feedback to
        be integrated at each sprint.
  \item \textbf{Visibility:} a prioritised backlog and bounded sprints
        provide a clear view of progress at any point.
  \item \textbf{Regular rhythm:} two-week sprints impose a structuring
        cadence well-suited to the internship calendar.
  \item \textbf{Frequent deliveries:} each sprint produces a
        functional increment, facilitating demonstrations and
        validations with the professional supervisor.
  \item \textbf{Industry adoption:} Scrum is the most widely practised
        agile framework, and its use in a PFE context prepares the
        student for professional practice.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Scrum Framework}

Scrum is an agile project management framework that helps teams
organise and supervise their work through values, principles, and
practices~\cite{scrumguide}. It encourages teams to learn from experience, self-organise
while working on a problem, and reflect on their successes and
failures to continuously improve.

Figure~\ref{fig:scrum} illustrates the Scrum process: successive
sprints feed functional increments into the product, validated at
each iteration by the Product Owner.

\subsection{Scrum Roles}

Scrum defines three complementary roles:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Product Owner:} represents the stakeholders and defines
        the product vision. He prioritises the Product Backlog items
        and validates each delivered increment. In this project, this
        role was fulfilled by \textbf{Mr.\ Mohammed Aziz Chibani}
        (NeuralBey), who approved the sprint backlog and conducted
        sprint reviews.
  \item \textbf{Scrum Master:} guarantees the application of the Scrum
        framework, facilitates team work, and removes obstacles. Given
        the single-developer nature of this PFE, this role was also
        assumed by Mr.\ Chibani, who provided architectural guidance
        and methodology coaching.
  \item \textbf{Development Team:} responsible for the technical
        realisation of features and the delivery of a potentially
        deployable increment at the end of each sprint. Composed in
        this project of the student, \textbf{Nadim Jebali}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scrum Events}

The Scrum framework organises work around five key events:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Sprint:} a fixed-duration iteration (two weeks in this
        project) during which the team produces a product increment.
  \item \textbf{Sprint Planning:} an opening meeting in which the team
        selects and plans the backlog items to realise during the
        sprint.
  \item \textbf{Daily Scrum:} a brief daily synchronisation of 15
        minutes to coordinate work and surface blockers.
  \item \textbf{Sprint Review:} a demonstration of the increment to
        stakeholders at the end of the sprint to collect feedback and
        validate progress.
  \item \textbf{Sprint Retrospective:} a process improvement meeting
        at the end of each sprint. Key improvements that emerged from
        retrospectives in this project include the adoption of
        \texttt{pnpm} for monorepo management, the migration from
        manual deployments to GitHub Actions, and the introduction of
        n8n for AI workflows.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Scrum Artefacts}

Three artefacts ensure transparency of the work:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Product Backlog:} a prioritised list of all expected
        product functionalities, maintained as user stories with
        MoSCoW priorities and complexity estimates (see
        Chapter~\ref{chap:requirements}).
  \item \textbf{Sprint Backlog:} a sub-set of the Product Backlog
        selected for a given sprint, broken down into technical tasks.
  \item \textbf{Increment:} a potentially deliverable version of the
        product obtained at the end of each sprint. The full project
        ran across approximately ten sprints, grouped into three
        milestones:
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Foundations (sprints 1--3):} repository setup,
        authentication with Better-Auth, base data model, and the
        seller-application flow.
  \item \textbf{Core marketplace (sprints 4--7):} stores, products,
        orders, admin panels, and tag-based recommendations.
  \item \textbf{Intelligence and delivery (sprints 8--10):} the AI
        verification pipeline, the in-app AI chat assistant,
        Dockerisation, Terraform provisioning, and the CI/CD
        pipeline.
\end{itemize}

\section{Identification of Actors}

The platform recognises three actor categories. They are distinct
\textit{roles} stored on the \texttt{User} table rather than
distinct user records --- a single account can be promoted from
\textit{customer} to \textit{seller} once a seller application is
approved, and an administrator account is created automatically on
first boot.

\subsection{Customer}

The Customer is the default role assigned upon registration. A
Customer can browse the catalogue, search products, view individual
stores, manage a cart, place an order across multiple sellers in a
single checkout, and review their personal order history. The
Customer is also the entry point for the seller pipeline: any
Customer can submit a seller application and, once approved, gain
the Seller role on the same account. Anonymous (non-authenticated)
visitors share the read-only abilities of the Customer but cannot
maintain a cart or place an order.

\subsection{Seller}

The Seller is a Customer whose seller application has been approved
by an administrator. A Seller can create and edit one store under
the free tier (or several under the premium tier), populate that
store with products, upload product images, attach product tags,
view per-store analytics, and consult the order history specific to
their store. Sellers also choose a visual template for their store
from the templates exposed by the frontend. They cannot, however,
review or moderate the work of other sellers.

\subsection{Administrator}

The Administrator is created automatically by the backend at first
boot and is not exposed through the registration form. The
Administrator reviews seller applications, browses every store and
every product on the platform, suspends or deletes stores (with
cascading deletion of dependent records), overrides automated AI
verification decisions, curates featured stores and products on the
landing page, manages categories and sub-categories, and consults
the platform through a database-aware AI chat assistant.

\section{Functional Requirements}
\label{sec:functional-requirements}

The functional requirements are grouped into seven domains. Each
domain corresponds to a NestJS module on the backend and to a
matching set of pages on the React frontend; the mapping is made
explicit in Chapter~3.

\subsection{User Registration and Authentication}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-A\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item The system shall allow a visitor to create an account with
        an email address and password.
  \item The system shall send a verification email containing a
        one-time link upon successful registration.
  \item The system shall prevent unverified accounts from creating a
        store or placing an order.
  \item The system shall authenticate returning users via Better-Auth
        sessions and issue an HTTP-only cookie.
  \item The system shall allow the user to log out and invalidate
        the corresponding session.
  \item The system shall enforce role-based access control on every
        non-public route through guards and the \texttt{@Roles()}
        decorator.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Store Management}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-S\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A Seller shall be able to create a store with a name,
        description, logo and template.
  \item A Seller shall be able to edit their store profile at any
        time.
  \item A free-tier Seller shall be limited to a single store.
  \item A premium-tier Seller shall be able to operate multiple
        stores from the same account.
  \item Each store shall expose a public storefront URL identified
        by a human-readable slug derived from the store name (e.g.\
        \texttt{/stores/pawsome-pet-foods}), rather than by its
        numeric primary key.
  \item Slug uniqueness shall be enforced at the database level and
        collisions resolved by an automatic numeric suffix
        (\texttt{-2}, \texttt{-3}, \ldots). The slug shall be
        regenerated when the store name changes.
  \item The store status (active, pending, suspended) shall be
        persisted in a \texttt{StoreStatusLog} audit trail.
  \item The Seller shall be able to customise the storefront across
        seven independent sections --- \textit{Theme},
        \textit{Announcement Bar}, \textit{Banner}, \textit{Header},
        \textit{Product Grid}, \textit{Sidebar} and \textit{Footer}
        --- through a WYSIWYG visual editor built with
        Craft.js, where clicking any section on the live canvas
        immediately opens its settings panel.
  \item Four pre-built presets shall be available as starting points
        for customisation: \textit{Default} and \textit{Minimal} are
        available to all sellers, while \textit{Modern} and
        \textit{Classic} are restricted to premium accounts. Applying
        a preset overwrites the current draft with the preset's
        section defaults.
  \item The Seller shall be able to upload a custom banner image,
        stored under \texttt{/uploads/stores/} and referenced from
        the customisation JSON.
  \item The customisation editor shall support undo/redo on every
        section change and shall warn the seller before discarding
        unsaved changes.
  \item Customisation features shall be tiered. Free accounts may
        use a curated subset --- a six-colour palette for the primary
        colour, two fonts (\textit{Inter} and the system default),
        the \textit{soft} corner radius, checkbox-style filters,
        square product images, and section visibility toggles.
        Premium accounts unlock the full set: free-form hex colours,
        all four fonts, all radii, banner image upload with heading
        and overlay controls, sidebar inner panel and text colours,
        non-checkbox filter styles, product grid background,
        non-square aspect ratios, and footer social links. When a
        premium subscription expires, the seller's existing
        customisation values are preserved at the database level and
        remain visible to visitors; the seller is only prevented from
        \emph{modifying} those premium fields going forward.
  \item A Seller shall be able to activate a store-wide sale by
        enabling a percentage discount (1--90\,\%), an optional custom
        label (e.g.\ \textit{"Summer Sale"}), and an optional expiry
        date. While the sale is active, every product in the store
        shall display its discounted price alongside a red sale badge
        on product cards, the store card, and search results. The
        discounted price shall also be used as the unit price when the
        product is added to an order.
  \item A Seller shall be able to trigger an AI-assisted full
        storefront redesign by submitting a short natural-language
        prompt. The system shall forward the request to an n8n
        workflow powered by the Groq \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile}
        model, which generates a complete \texttt{StoreCustomization}
        object covering all seven sections. The result shall be applied
        to the live Craft.js editor immediately, allowing the seller
        to review or further refine the design before saving.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Product Management}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-P\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A Seller shall be able to create, edit and delete products
        belonging to their own store(s).
  \item Each product shall accept one or more images of supported
        MIME types, with a configurable maximum file size.
  \item Each product shall support an arbitrary number of tags used
        by the recommendation engine.
  \item Each product shall carry two review flags: \texttt{aiReviewed},
        set automatically by the AI verification pipeline, and
        \texttt{adminReviewed}, set when an Administrator manually
        overrides the AI verdict.
  \item Products shall be browsable by category and sub-category.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Order System}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-O\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A Customer shall be able to add and remove items in a cart
        persisted on the client side.
  \item Checkout shall create a single \texttt{Order} entity even
        when the cart spans multiple stores, with one
        \texttt{OrderItem} per product line.
  \item Order placement shall trigger a confirmation email through
        Nodemailer.
  \item Each Customer shall be able to consult their personal order
        history.
  \item Each Seller shall be able to consult the orders that include
        at least one product from their store.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Admin Dashboard}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-D\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item The Administrator shall access a dashboard listing pending
        seller applications, recent activity and platform-wide
        statistics.
  \item The Administrator shall be able to approve or reject seller
        applications, granting the Seller role on approval.
  \item The Administrator shall be able to suspend, reactivate or
        delete any store, with cascading deletion of its products
        and orders.
  \item The Administrator shall be able to override the
        \texttt{adminReviewed} verdict of any product or store.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{AI Verification}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-V\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Once its images have been uploaded, every store and product
        shall be submitted to an n8n workflow
        (\texttt{cheebo-ai-verify}) that screens each image, together
        with the listing text, using a hosted multimodal
        large-language model.
  \item The workflow shall return a decision (\textit{approve} or
        \textit{flag}) with a textual rationale --- flagging the
        entity if \emph{any} of its images is inappropriate --- written
        back to the entity through a callback endpoint.
  \item Flagged entities shall be listed in the moderation queue
        for administrative review.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Featured Content Curation}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{FR-C\arabic*.},leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item The Administrator shall be able to mark any store or product
        as \textit{featured}.
  \item Featured entities shall be promoted on the landing page.
  \item In the absence of featured products for a given tag, the
        recommendation engine shall fall back to top-selling
        products of that tag.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Modelling Language}

This project adopts the \textbf{Unified Modelling Language (UML)} to
formalise both the static structure and the dynamic behaviour of the
system. UML provides a standardised set of diagrams that represent
software systems in a language-independent and tool-independent way.
Three diagram types are used throughout this report:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=4pt]
  \item \textbf{Use Case diagrams} --- describe the interactions
        between actors (Customer, Seller, Administrator) and the
        system, identifying the boundaries of the platform's
        functionality.
  \item \textbf{Sequence diagrams} --- model the temporal exchanges
        of messages between system components for the three
        load-bearing flows: authentication, order placement, and
        AI-driven product verification.
  \item \textbf{Class diagrams} --- represent the main domain entities,
        their attributes, and their associations, providing the
        structural blueprint from which the Prisma schema is derived.
\end{itemize}

\section{Global Architecture}

\subsection{3-Tier Architecture Overview}

Cheebo follows a classic \textbf{three-tier architecture} that separates
the user interface (presentation), the business logic (application), and
the persistent data (data) into three independent layers. This separation
is the structural foundation on which every other design decision in this
chapter builds: it lets each tier evolve at its own pace, scales the
backend horizontally without touching the frontend, and keeps the database
schema decoupled from the public REST contract.

Figure~\ref{fig:arch-global} shows the three tiers and the auxiliary
components that orbit them. End users reach the platform exclusively
over HTTPS, terminated by a \textbf{Caddy} reverse proxy that obtains
and renews its TLS certificate automatically from Let's Encrypt. Caddy
forwards the decrypted traffic to an internal \textbf{Nginx} container,
which serves the static React bundle and forwards every
\texttt{/api/*} request to the NestJS API container over the Docker
bridge network. The NestJS
application implements the entire business logic --- authentication,
catalogue management, order processing, moderation --- and persists state
to a single \textbf{MariaDB 11}~\cite{mariadb} instance through the
\textbf{Prisma} ORM~\cite{prisma}.
File uploads are handled by \textbf{Multer} and stored on a Docker
volume; transactional emails are dispatched through \textbf{Nodemailer}
over SMTP.

Sitting alongside the backend rather than inside it, \textbf{n8n} hosts
the three AI workflows (verification, database chat, AI redesign). The
backend reaches n8n via internal HTTP webhooks; n8n in turn calls the
LLM providers (Groq and Gemini) over the public internet. This
separation isolates AI orchestration --- prompt templates, model
selection, retry logic --- from the core business logic, so it can be
modified through the n8n editor without redeploying the API.

Figure~\ref{fig:arch-global} summarises the topology. End users on
the left reach Nginx, which serves the SPA and forwards API traffic
to the NestJS backend; the backend in turn talks to MariaDB,
Nodemailer and Multer, and exchanges webhooks with n8n on the right,
which itself calls the LLM providers.

\subsection{n8n as an Automation Layer}

n8n~\cite{n8n} was introduced as a deliberate architectural choice rather than an
ad-hoc tool. Three properties made it the right fit for Cheebo's AI
features:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Workflow-as-data.} Every AI feature is expressed as a
        JSON workflow that can be exported, version-controlled in the
        repository (\texttt{n8n-workflows/}), and re-imported on any
        n8n instance. The workflow becomes a first-class artefact of
        the system, reviewed alongside the application code.
  \item \textbf{Provider abstraction.} The NestJS backend never imports
        an LLM SDK. Switching from Gemini to Groq, or adding a new
        provider for a specific node, is a configuration change inside
        the n8n editor with no NestJS recompilation. This decoupling
        also keeps API keys out of the backend container.
  \item \textbf{Native webhook orientation.} n8n exposes any workflow as
        a webhook endpoint (\texttt{/webhook/<name>}) and supports
        synchronous \emph{Respond to Webhook} for request/response flows
        and asynchronous patterns for fire-and-forget jobs. This matches
        exactly the three integration patterns Cheebo needs:
        asynchronous (verification fires on entity creation and writes
        back later), synchronous (AI redesign returns the
        \texttt{StoreCustomization} to the seller's editor in the same
        call), and conversational (the database chat assistant streams
        messages between the admin and the LLM).
\end{enumerate}

The trade-off accepted in exchange for these benefits is one additional
container in the production stack and one additional persistence volume
(\texttt{n8n\_data}) to back up. Both are justified by the maintainability
gains.

\section{Detailed Technical Architecture}

\subsection{NestJS Modular Architecture}

The backend is structured as a set of \textbf{feature
modules}~\cite{nestjs}, each
encapsulating its own controller, service, DTOs, and Prisma queries.
Every module declares its own \texttt{@Module()} decorator and exports
the providers consumed by sibling modules; this strict separation is
what allows the dependency graph in Figure~\ref{fig:arch-nest} to remain
acyclic in a system that hosts almost twenty modules.

\paragraph{Cross-cutting modules.}
Four modules are imported by virtually every feature:
\texttt{PrismaModule} exposes the singleton Prisma client;
\texttt{AuthModule} provides the \texttt{AuthGuard} and the
\texttt{@Roles()} decorator that gate every protected route;
\texttt{NotificationModule} centralises in-app notifications dispatched
through the EventEmitter;
\texttt{SettingsModule} stores platform-wide configuration that
administrators can update without redeploying.

\paragraph{Feature modules grouped by domain.}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{User and application:} \texttt{UserModule} and
        \texttt{SellerApplicationModule} handle profile management
        and the seller onboarding workflow.
  \item \textbf{Storefront:} \texttt{StoreModule}, \texttt{StoreStatusLogModule},
        \texttt{ProductModule}, \texttt{ProductImageModule}, and
        \texttt{ProductTagModule} cover everything a seller owns.
  \item \textbf{Catalogue:} \texttt{CategoryModule} and
        \texttt{SubCategoryModule} maintain the taxonomy that the
        product grid filters by.
  \item \textbf{Commerce:} \texttt{OrderModule} and
        \texttt{OrderItemsModule} create orders and persist line items
        with the discounted unit price computed at checkout.
  \item \textbf{Discovery and analytics:} \texttt{HomeModule} powers
        the landing page (featured stores, featured products, tag-based
        recommendations); \texttt{AnalyticsModule} computes per-store
        revenue and visitor metrics.
  \item \textbf{AI integration:} \texttt{ChatModule} relays
        administrator messages to the database chat workflow;
        \texttt{N8nModule} centralises the webhook calls used by both
        the verification pipeline and the AI redesign feature.
\end{itemize}

This layout maps one-to-one onto the functional requirement domains of
Section~\ref{sec:functional-requirements}, which makes it easy to
trace a requirement to a single module and vice versa.

Figure~\ref{fig:arch-nest} visualises the resulting module catalogue.
The cross-cutting providers are shown in blue at the top, the
domain-specific feature modules are grouped into five coloured packages,
and the AI integration package on the right collects the modules that
exchange webhooks with n8n.

\subsection{React SPA Architecture}

The frontend is a \textbf{Single-Page Application} built with
React~19~\cite{react}
and bundled by \textbf{Vite}~\cite{vite}. The codebase under
\texttt{frontend/src/} is split into seven top-level folders, each
playing a clearly defined role:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{pages/} --- one file per route, including the public
        catalogue (\texttt{Home}, \texttt{ProductsPage},
        \texttt{StorePage}, \texttt{ProductPage}), the checkout flow
        (\texttt{CartPage}, \texttt{CheckoutPage},
        \texttt{OrderSuccessPage}), authentication
        (\texttt{Login}, \texttt{Register},
        \texttt{EmailVerified}), and a nested
        \texttt{dashboard/} subtree split by role (seller, admin).
  \item \texttt{components/} --- reusable building blocks
        (\texttt{ProductCard}, \texttt{StoreCard},
        \texttt{SearchBar}, \texttt{AiChatFab}, and the
        \texttt{ui/} folder of \texttt{shadcn/ui} primitives).
  \item \texttt{api/} --- one TypeScript file per backend module
        (\texttt{store.api.ts}, \texttt{product.api.ts}, \ldots)
        that wraps the typed Axios client and exposes a single
        object of named functions, mirroring the NestJS layout.
  \item \texttt{stores/} --- Zustand stores for client-side state
        (\texttt{auth.store}, \texttt{cart.store},
        \texttt{admin.store}, \texttt{category.store},
        \texttt{customization.editor.store}).
  \item \texttt{hooks/} --- TanStack React Query hooks that wrap
        the API layer, providing caching, deduplication, and
        background refetching.
  \item \texttt{lib/} --- framework-agnostic utilities (Axios
        instance, customisation hydration, error formatter).
  \item \texttt{types/} and \texttt{constants/} --- domain types
        shared with the backend DTOs and constant tables (roles,
        statuses, MIME lists).
\end{itemize}

State management follows a strict two-tier convention: \textbf{server
state} (anything originating from the API) lives in \textbf{TanStack
Query} caches keyed by the API path; \textbf{client-only state} (cart
contents, theme preference, draft store customisation in the WYSIWYG
editor) lives in \textbf{Zustand} stores. This division eliminates the
classic React anti-pattern of duplicating server data into a Redux
store and then fighting cache invalidation.

The storefront editor itself is a separate sub-architecture built on
\textbf{Craft.js}~\cite{craftjs}: the canvas, the section palette, and the
inspector panel are wired around a single \texttt{<Editor>} provider,
and a Zustand store mirrors the Craft editor state so that the
\textit{Save} button can submit a stable JSON payload to the
backend.

\subsection{Docker Compose Services Diagram}

The full application stack is defined in a single
\texttt{docker-compose.yml} file at the root of the monorepo. Five
services are declared (Figure~\ref{fig:docker-services}):

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{db} --- official \texttt{mariadb:11} image with a
        named volume \texttt{db\_data} and a health-check that
        gates the backend's start-up.
  \item \textbf{backend} --- the pre-built
        \texttt{sirvinny/cheebo-backend:latest} image, configured
        from environment variables (database credentials,
        Better-Auth secret, SMTP settings, n8n webhook URLs,
        premium-tier limits) and mounting an \texttt{uploads}
        volume for stored images.
  \item \textbf{frontend} --- the pre-built
        \texttt{sirvinny/cheebo-frontend:latest} image (Nginx
        serving the React bundle). It exposes no host port and is
        reached only through the Caddy proxy.
  \item \textbf{caddy} --- the official \texttt{caddy:2-alpine}
        image, the public entrypoint of the whole stack. It is the
        only service that publishes host ports (\texttt{80} and
        \texttt{443}), terminates HTTPS with an automatically issued
        Let's Encrypt certificate, and reverse-proxies to the
        internal Nginx frontend. Its certificate and state persist in
        the \texttt{caddy\_data} and \texttt{caddy\_config} volumes.
  \item \textbf{n8n} --- the official \texttt{n8nio/n8n:latest}
        image with a named volume \texttt{n8n\_data} preserving
        workflows and credentials across restarts.
\end{itemize}

All services share a single Docker bridge network that is created
implicitly by Compose; only Caddy's ports are published. The
frontend, backend, database, and n8n are never reachable directly
from the public internet --- the backend's URL is
\texttt{http://backend:3000} from inside the bridge network, and the
database is reachable as \texttt{db:3306}. This single-host topology
is intentional: the production droplet specifications
(Section~\ref{sec:droplet-specs}) size for it, and the architecture is
straightforward to migrate to a managed database or a Kubernetes
deployment later.

Figure~\ref{fig:docker-services} shows the resulting topology on the
production host: the containers sit on a shared Docker bridge
network, only Caddy publishes ports to the public internet, and each
stateful service writes to its own named volume.

authentication (\texttt{Session}, \texttt{Account},
\texttt{Verification}), commerce (\texttt{Order} and through it
\texttt{OrderItems}), and storefront ownership
(\texttt{SellerApplication} and \texttt{Store}). Each \texttt{Store}
in turn owns its own subtree of \texttt{Product}, \texttt{ProductImage},
\texttt{ProductTag} and \texttt{StoreStatusLog} records, while the
\texttt{Category}/\texttt{Subcategory} hierarchy lives orthogonally and
is referenced by every product.

\subsection{Prisma Schema Overview}

The Prisma schema is split into eighteen files under
\texttt{backend/prisma/schema/}, one per logical entity, plus an
\texttt{enums/} folder gathering every enumeration in a single place.
This split takes advantage of the \texttt{multi-file schema} preview
feature introduced in Prisma~5 and keeps each file under one screen,
which makes diff review on pull requests far easier than a single
multi-thousand-line schema.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Authentication} (\texttt{user.prisma},
        \texttt{auth.prisma}) --- four models: \texttt{User},
        \texttt{Session}, \texttt{Account}, \texttt{Verification}.
        The non-\texttt{User} tables are managed entirely by
        Better-Auth and are exposed to Prisma read-only.
  \item \textbf{Storefront} (\texttt{store.prisma},
        \texttt{store-status-log.prisma},
        \texttt{store-template.prisma}) --- the \texttt{Store} model
        carries a JSON \texttt{customization} column that holds the
        Craft.js editor state, plus the four sale fields
        (\texttt{saleEnabled}, \texttt{saleDiscountPercent},
        \texttt{saleLabel}, \texttt{saleEndsAt}).
  \item \textbf{Product catalogue} (\texttt{product.prisma},
        \texttt{product-image.prisma}, \texttt{product-tag.prisma},
        \texttt{category.prisma}, \texttt{subcategory.prisma}) ---
        five models capturing the catalogue, with two-level
        category taxonomy and many-to-many tags via an implicit
        join table.
  \item \textbf{Commerce} (\texttt{order.prisma},
        \texttt{order-items.prisma}) --- two models with the unit
        price persisted on each line so that subsequent price
        changes never alter historical orders.
  \item \textbf{Curation} (\texttt{featured.prisma}) --- two
        models (\texttt{FeaturedStore}, \texttt{FeaturedProduct})
        with a \texttt{position} integer driving the landing-page
        ordering.
  \item \textbf{Workflow} (\texttt{seller-application.prisma},
        \texttt{notification.prisma}, \texttt{setting.prisma})
        --- application reviews, in-app notifications, and
        platform-wide settings respectively.
\end{itemize}

The complete schema, including indexes and cascade rules, is
reproduced in Appendix~\ref{app:prisma-schema}.

\subsection{Explanation of Key Relationships}

A handful of relationships carry most of the load and deserve a
dedicated note.

\paragraph{User $\rightarrow$ Store $\rightarrow$ Product (1:N:N).}
A \texttt{User} with the \texttt{SELLER} role can own one store on
the free tier and several on the premium tier; each store owns an
unbounded number of products. The chain is materialised by two
foreign keys (\texttt{Store.userId}, \texttt{Product.storeId})
both indexed. The cascading-delete rule on \texttt{Product} ensures
that suspending or deleting a store cleanly removes the products it
contained.

\paragraph{User $\leftrightarrow$ SellerApplication (1:0..1).}
A user has at most one open seller application at any time. The
\texttt{userId} column is \texttt{@unique}, which prevents duplicate
submissions at the database level rather than at the service layer.
The \texttt{Cascade} delete rule on the FK ensures that deleting a
user automatically purges the application row.

\paragraph{Order $\rightarrow$ Store and Order $\rightarrow$ OrderItems.}
A single multi-store checkout creates \textbf{one \texttt{Order} per
store} (so the seller has a clean per-store view) but a single
shopping flow for the customer. Each \texttt{OrderItems} row carries
the unit price that was visible at checkout time --- this is the
discounted price when the store had an active sale, computed by the
\texttt{Product} service before the order is persisted. Subsequent
price changes never rewrite history.

\paragraph{Product $\leftrightarrow$ ProductTag (M:N).}
Prisma's \emph{implicit} many-to-many relation is used here: Prisma
manages an internal join table \texttt{\_ProductTags} that does not
appear in the schema file, which keeps the model lean while still
allowing efficient queries by tag for the recommendation engine.

\paragraph{Verification flags on Store and Product.}
Both entities carry two independent boolean flags:
\texttt{aiReviewed} (set automatically by the n8n verification
workflow) and \texttt{adminReviewed} (set when an administrator
manually overrides the AI verdict). This dual-flag design preserves
the audit trail of who --- machine or human --- approved a piece of
content, and lets the moderation queue filter on
\texttt{aiReviewed = false AND adminReviewed = false} to find
genuinely pending items.

\paragraph{Sale fields on Store.}
The four sale fields live on \texttt{Store} rather than on a
separate \texttt{Sale} entity. This denormalisation is deliberate:
sales are always store-wide, only one is active at a time, and the
fields are read on every product query. A separate join would have
added a query without buying any expressive power.

\paragraph{Payment fields on Order.}
Because checkout is confirmed asynchronously by Stripe, the
\texttt{Order} entity carries its own payment state rather than
overloading the fulfilment \texttt{status}. A \texttt{PaymentStatus}
enum (\texttt{UNPAID}, \texttt{PAID}, \texttt{FAILED}) records where
the charge stands, \texttt{stripePaymentIntentId} links the order back
to the Stripe \texttt{PaymentIntent} that covers it, and
\texttt{paidAt} stamps the moment payment succeeded. A single intent
can cover several orders of a multi-store cart, so that column is a
\emph{non-unique} index rather than a unique key. An order is created
\texttt{UNPAID} and only a verified Stripe webhook --- never the
client --- moves it to \texttt{PAID}, as detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:backend-payments}.

\section{API Design}

\subsection{REST API Design Principles}

The Cheebo backend exposes a single \textbf{REST API}~\cite{fielding}
over JSON
that the React SPA consumes through a typed Axios client. Six
principles guided every route declaration:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Resource-oriented URLs.} Routes name nouns
        (\texttt{/store}, \texttt{/product}, \texttt{/order})
        and use sub-paths for actions on a specific resource
        (\texttt{/store/:id/sale},
        \texttt{/store/:id/ai-redesign}). Verbs in URLs are
        avoided.
  \item \textbf{HTTP verb semantics.} \texttt{GET} for safe
        reads, \texttt{POST} for creation, \texttt{PATCH} for
        partial updates, \texttt{PUT} for full replacements,
        \texttt{DELETE} for removal. Status codes follow the
        same convention --- \texttt{201 Created} on
        \texttt{POST}, \texttt{204 No Content} on
        \texttt{DELETE}, \texttt{400} for validation errors,
        \texttt{401} for unauthenticated access, \texttt{403}
        for role violations~\cite{mdn}.
  \item \textbf{Validated DTOs.} Every body- or query-bearing
        endpoint declares a \texttt{class-validator} DTO with
        \texttt{forbidNonWhitelisted: true}, so any extra field
        in the payload is rejected at the framework layer
        before the controller handler executes.
  \item \textbf{Role-based access control.} Protected routes
        are decorated with \texttt{@UseGuards(AuthGuard,
        RolesGuard)} and the \texttt{@Roles(\ldots)} decorator
        listing the allowed roles. Anonymous endpoints (catalogue
        browsing, store page) carry \texttt{@Public()}.
  \item \textbf{Cursor- and page-based pagination.} List
        endpoints accept \texttt{page} and \texttt{limit} query
        parameters and always return a uniform
        \texttt{PaginatedResult<T>} envelope
        (\texttt{\{ data, total, page, limit \}}).
  \item \textbf{Stable error contract.} Failures are
        serialised as
        \texttt{\{ statusCode, message, error \}} so that the
        frontend's \texttt{getApiError} helper can handle both
        single-string and array-of-strings messages from
        validation pipes without branching on the endpoint.
\end{enumerate}

OpenAPI documentation is generated automatically by the
\texttt{@nestjs/swagger} module and served at
\texttt{/api/docs} when the \texttt{SWAGGER\_PATH} environment
variable is set, which is useful in development and during
defence preparation but disabled in production.

\subsection{Authentication Flow (Better-Auth)}

Authentication is handled entirely by the \textbf{Better-Auth}
library~\cite{betterauth}, integrated into NestJS through a thin adapter module.
Better-Auth was preferred over rolling a custom Passport
strategy because it ships with email/password, email
verification, session persistence in the database, and
cookie-based session transport out of the box --- everything
Cheebo needs without writing any cryptography. Sessions are
opaque DB-backed tokens (not JWTs), which makes server-side
revocation a one-line update on the \texttt{Session} table.

The interaction was already presented as a sequence diagram in
Chapter~\ref{chap:requirements} (Figure~\ref{fig:seq-auth}); from the
design standpoint it is a three-stage flow:
registration (creates the \texttt{User} row, sends the
verification email, returns a session cookie marked
\emph{pending verification}), email verification (flips
\texttt{emailVerified=true}), and login (validates credentials
and issues a fresh session). Every subsequent protected
request carries the \texttt{better-auth.session\_token} cookie,
which the NestJS \texttt{AuthGuard} validates against the
\texttt{Session} table on each call.

\section{n8n Workflow Design}

Cheebo's AI features are carried by three independent n8n workflows
deployed on the same engine. Each one is exposed as an HTTP webhook
that the NestJS backend invokes; their JSON definitions live in
\texttt{n8n-workflows/} in the repository and are reproduced in
Appendix~\ref{app:n8n-workflows}. The three workflows differ in
their trigger, integration pattern, and downstream model:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{AI verification} --- fired once a seller has uploaded
        the images for a new store or product; screens every image
        with a multimodal model, asynchronous response through a
        callback endpoint.
  \item \textbf{Database chat assistant} --- triggered by an
        administrator's chat message, conversational
        request/response.
  \item \textbf{AI storefront redesign} --- triggered explicitly
        by a seller from the Craft.js editor, synchronous response
        returning a \texttt{StoreCustomization} JSON object.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{AI Verification Workflow}

Figure~\ref{fig:n8n-verify} shows the n8n workflow responsible for
store and product verification. Once the entity's images have been
uploaded, the backend fires the \texttt{cheebo-ai-verify} webhook;
the workflow fans the image list out into one branch per image,
downloads each file from the backend's \texttt{/uploads} route, and
submits it together with the listing text to the Gemini vision
model. The per-image verdicts are then aggregated --- the entity is
flagged if \emph{any} image is inappropriate --- and the combined
decision is posted back to the \texttt{/ai-review} callback endpoint.

The third n8n workflow handles AI-assisted storefront redesign.

entity creation, this workflow is initiated explicitly by the seller
from the Craft.js editor. The seller provides a short prompt describing
the desired aesthetic; the backend forwards it to the
\textbf{cheebo-store-redesign} webhook along with the store name and
description as context.

The workflow is composed of three nodes: a \textbf{Build Prompt} Code
node that assembles the system instructions and enforces the
\texttt{StoreCustomization} JSON schema; an \textbf{AI Agent} node
connected to the Groq \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} model that
produces the design; and a \textbf{Parse \& Sanitise} Code node that
validates hex colour values, clamps numeric fields, and applies safe
defaults for any missing properties. The sanitised object is returned
synchronously to the backend, which persists it and streams it back to
the editor.

\section{Class Diagram}

Figure~\ref{fig:class-diagram} presents the UML class diagram of the
main domain entities. Where the ER diagram of
Section~\ref{sec:er-diagram} focused on tables, foreign keys and
cardinalities, the class diagram emphasises the \emph{behaviour}
attached to each entity --- the methods invoked by the service layer
during use case execution --- and the \emph{enumerations} that
constrain the state of every long-lived object.

Three structural relationships dominate the model:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{User-driven aggregation.} The \texttt{User} class
        plays the role of the application's principal aggregate. It
        has a 1:0..1 association with \texttt{SellerApplication}, a
        1:N composition with \texttt{Store} (one user, multiple
        stores on the premium tier), and a 1:N association with
        \texttt{Order} as a customer.
  \item \textbf{Store/Product composition.} \texttt{Store} composes
        \texttt{Product} (filled diamond): products cannot exist
        independently of their store, and cascading deletes are
        enforced at both the Prisma and database levels. The same
        composition links \texttt{Product} to its
        \texttt{ProductImage} children.
  \item \textbf{Order/OrderItems aggregation.} \texttt{Order}
        aggregates \texttt{OrderItems} (open diamond): order lines
        always belong to exactly one order, but the
        \texttt{Product} they reference has an independent
        lifecycle from the order. This is what allows historical
        orders to keep displaying a product that has since been
        deleted from a store.
\end{itemize}

Four enumerations (\texttt{UserRole}, \texttt{StoreStatus},
\texttt{OrderStatus}, \texttt{VerificationStatus}) constrain the
state machines of their owning entities. Methods shown on each
class correspond directly to service-layer operations exposed by
the matching NestJS module --- \texttt{Store.suspend(reason)}
maps to \texttt{StoreService.suspend()}, \texttt{Product.\allowbreak%
computeSalePrice()} maps to the helper used by every product
query, and so on.

Figure~\ref{fig:class-diagram} renders the resulting model.
Aggregate roots (\texttt{User}, \texttt{Store}, \texttt{Order})
appear with their associations and the multiplicities that govern
them; supporting classes (\texttt{ProductImage},
\texttt{ProductTag}, \texttt{OrderItems}) sit alongside the entity
they belong to; and the four enumerations are linked to their
owning classes through dashed dependency arrows.

\begin{keytakeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item The system is a three-tier architecture --- React SPA,
        NestJS API, MariaDB --- with n8n as an external automation
        layer reached only through webhooks.
  \item The NestJS backend is composed of around twenty feature
        modules grouped into six functional clusters
        (cross-cutting, user, storefront, catalogue, commerce,
        AI), each mapping one-to-one to a functional requirement
        domain.
  \item The Prisma schema is split into eighteen files for
        reviewability, and uses the JSON \texttt{customization}
        column on \texttt{Store} to persist the Craft.js editor
        state without proliferating tables.
  \item The REST API follows resource-oriented design, validates
        every payload with \texttt{class-validator} DTOs and
        enforces RBAC through the \texttt{@Roles()} decorator.
  \item Three independent n8n workflows --- verification, DB
        chat, storefront redesign --- isolate prompt and provider
        choices from the backend.
\end{itemize}
\end{keytakeaways}

\section{Backend (NestJS)}

\subsection{Project Structure}

The backend follows the conventional NestJS layout: a thin
\texttt{main.ts} bootstrap that validates the required environment at
start-up (fail-fast), applies the Helmet security-header middleware and
the global \texttt{ValidationPipe}, and mounts the Swagger document only
outside production, a root
\texttt{AppModule} that imports every feature module, and one
directory per domain under \texttt{src/}. Each feature directory is
self-contained and groups its \texttt{*.module.ts},
\texttt{*.controller.ts}, \texttt{*.service.ts}, a \texttt{dto/}
folder of request/response DTOs, and --- as detailed in
Section~\ref{sec:tests} --- its co-located \texttt{*.spec.ts} tests. Cross-cutting
concerns live under \texttt{src/common/} (guards, pagination DTOs,
the Multer configuration, customization helpers) and
\texttt{src/prisma/} (the database client wrapper). The Prisma client
is code-generated into \texttt{src/generated/prisma/} and imported as
a normal TypeScript module.

This per-domain split means a change to, say, the order flow touches
only the \texttt{order/} directory, and the NestJS dependency
injection container resolves a service's collaborators
(\texttt{PrismaService}, \texttt{NotificationService}) by their
injection tokens rather than by file path --- which is exactly what
makes the services straightforward to unit-test with mocked
collaborators.

\subsection{Key Modules}

The \texttt{AppModule} composes eighteen feature modules. The most
load-bearing ones are described below; their dependency relationships
were presented as the module-cluster diagram in Chapter~3.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{AuthModule} --- wraps Better-Auth, exposes the
        session endpoints, and provides the \texttt{AuthGuard} used
        by every protected route. Session tokens are database-backed
        rather than stateless JWTs.
  \item \textbf{StoreModule} --- store CRUD, slug generation,
        premium-template gating, the store-wide sale feature, the
        Craft.js customization JSON, and the AI-redesign webhook
        call. It is the largest single domain in the codebase.
  \item \textbf{ProductModule} --- product CRUD, the free-tier
        product cap, sale-price computation
        (\texttt{applySalePrices}), AI/admin verification flags, and
        tag-based recommendations.
  \item \textbf{OrderModule} / \textbf{OrderItemsModule} --- order
        creation, per-seller dashboard scoping, and transactional
        order-status emails.
  \item \textbf{SellerApplicationModule} --- the seller-onboarding
        state machine that promotes an approved applicant to the
        \texttt{SELLER} role.
  \item \textbf{NotificationModule} --- persists notifications,
        fans out to all admins, and streams live updates to connected
        clients over Server-Sent Events.
  \item \textbf{ChatModule} and \textbf{N8nModule} --- the bridge to
        the n8n automation layer: the database chat assistant and the
        AI verification/redesign webhooks.
  \item \textbf{PaymentModule} --- Stripe checkout, webhook-confirmed
        order payment, and the scheduled stale-checkout reconciliation
        sweep that keeps reserved stock and payment state consistent.
  \item \textbf{PrismaModule} --- a global module exporting the
        single \texttt{PrismaService} instance injected everywhere
        else.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Authentication Guards and Role-Based Access Control}

Every protected route is secured through a combination of
\texttt{AuthGuard} and the \texttt{@Roles()} decorator.
Figure~\ref{fig:rbac} shows a representative controller method
annotated with both guards, illustrating how the framework enforces
role-based access at the route level before the handler executes.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \imagePlaceholder[5cm]{Code snippet — AuthGuard + @Roles() decorator}
  \caption{Role-based access control implementation}
  \label{fig:rbac}
\end{figure}

Role gating alone is not sufficient, because two sellers share the
same \texttt{SELLER} role yet must not be able to edit each other's
content. Authorization is therefore enforced at two levels. The
\texttt{AuthGuard} (and \texttt{@Roles()}) establishes \emph{who} the
caller is and \emph{which role} they hold; the service layer then
enforces \emph{object-level ownership} before any mutation. Updating or
deleting a product, uploading a store image, or reading and modifying
an order all resolve the owning user
(\texttt{Product\,$\rightarrow$\,Store.userId}; an order's customer or
owning store) and reject the request with \texttt{403 Forbidden} when
the caller is neither that owner nor an administrator. This closes the insecure-direct-object-reference
gap that role checks by themselves leave open, and is pinned by the
ownership and authorization unit tests of Section~\ref{sec:tests}.

\subsection{File Upload System (Multer)}

Image uploads --- store logos, store thumbnails, banner images, and
product images --- are handled by Multer through a single shared
factory, \texttt{multerStorage(destination)}, defined in
\texttt{src/common/multer/}. The factory returns a \texttt{diskStorage}
configuration that writes files under the configured uploads
directory, renaming each to a collision-proof
\texttt{<timestamp>-<random><ext>} name so that two users uploading
\texttt{logo.png} never overwrite one another.

Two guards are enforced at the Multer layer before a file ever
reaches a controller:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{MIME-type allow-list} --- only the types in
        \texttt{ALLOWED\_UPLOAD\_MIME\_TYPES} (default
        \texttt{jpeg}, \texttt{png}, \texttt{webp}, \texttt{gif})
        are accepted; anything else is rejected with a
        \texttt{BadRequestException}.
  \item \textbf{Size cap} --- a per-file limit of
        \texttt{MAX\_UPLOAD\_FILE\_SIZE\_MB} (default 5~MB),
        enforced by Multer's \texttt{limits} option.
\end{itemize}

Controllers consume the factory through NestJS's
\texttt{FileInterceptor}, for example on
\texttt{POST /product/:id/image}. The uploaded file's URL is then
persisted (as a \texttt{ProductImage} row or on the store record) and
the static file is served back by the Express static-assets
middleware configured in \texttt{main.ts}. Because the destination
directory is a Docker named volume, uploaded media survives container
re-creation (Section~\ref{sec:droplet-specs}).

\subsection{Email System (Nodemailer)}

Transactional email is sent through a single thin helper,
\texttt{sendEmail(\{ to, subject, html \})}, in
\texttt{src/auth/mailer.ts}. It builds a Nodemailer SMTP transport
from environment variables (\texttt{SMTP\_HOST},
\texttt{SMTP\_PORT}, \texttt{SMTP\_SECURE}, \texttt{SMTP\_USER},
\texttt{SMTP\_PASS}, \texttt{SMTP\_FROM}) with conservative
connection, greeting, and socket timeouts so that a slow mail server
cannot stall a request thread.

Two flows rely on it. First, Better-Auth uses it for
\textbf{email-verification} links during registration. Second, the
\texttt{OrderService} sends an \textbf{order-status email} whenever an
order transitions to a new status (confirmed, shipped, delivered,
cancelled, refunded), rendering a small HTML template with the order
number, store name, and a status-coloured badge. Crucially, these
emails are dispatched as fire-and-forget promises: a failure to send
is logged but never propagated to the caller, so a downed SMTP server
degrades gracefully (the order is still created) rather than failing
the user's request. This is precisely the behaviour pinned by the
\texttt{order.service} tests in Section~\ref{sec:tests}.

\subsection{Payment Processing (Stripe)}
\label{sec:backend-payments}

Checkout is handled by a dedicated \texttt{PaymentModule} built around
two collaborators: a thin \texttt{StripeService} that owns the single
Stripe client (created lazily, so the application still boots without
payment keys in development), and a \texttt{PaymentService} that
orchestrates the flow. The design follows one rule end-to-end: the
amount charged is always derived from the orders the server just
priced, never from any figure the client sends.

A checkout proceeds in three steps:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Reserve and price.}
        \texttt{OrderService.createPendingForCart} runs inside a single
        transaction, decrements stock atomically, and creates one
        \texttt{UNPAID} order per store, returning the server-computed
        grand total.
  \item \textbf{Open a PaymentIntent.} \texttt{PaymentService} opens a
        single Stripe \texttt{PaymentIntent} for that total. Because a
        Stripe account cannot settle in Tunisian dinar, the TND total
        is converted to US-dollar minor units through a configurable
        \texttt{TND\_TO\_USD\_RATE} (the helper
        \texttt{tndToStripeUsdMinor}); the interface keeps displaying
        TND while the charge is in USD. The order identifiers are
        written into the intent metadata, an \texttt{idempotencyKey}
        derived from them prevents a duplicate charge on retry, and the
        intent id is stamped back onto the orders. If the intent cannot
        be opened, the stock reserved a moment earlier is released so it
        is never stranded on a dead order.
  \item \textbf{Confirm by webhook.} The client confirms the intent
        with Stripe directly; the orders stay \texttt{UNPAID} until
        Stripe calls back. The \texttt{POST /payments/webhook} endpoint
        is unauthenticated by design --- trust comes from verifying the
        Stripe signature against the \emph{raw} request body (the
        \texttt{main.ts} bootstrap enables \texttt{rawBody} for exactly
        this), not from a session. On a verified
        \texttt{payment\_intent.succeeded} it marks the orders
        \texttt{PAID} and \texttt{CONFIRMED}; a failure or cancellation
        releases the reserved stock instead.
\end{itemize}

Both state transitions (\texttt{markPaid} and
\texttt{markFailedAndRestock}) are scoped to orders that are still
\texttt{UNPAID}, which makes them idempotent: Stripe may redeliver the
same event and the second delivery is a harmless no-op. The conversion
helper and the full webhook decision table are pinned by the
\texttt{payment.service} and \texttt{stripe-amount} unit tests of
Section~\ref{sec:tests}.

\subsection{Rate Limiting}

To blunt brute-force and abuse, the API applies a global rate limit
through \texttt{@nestjs/throttler}. \texttt{ThrottlerModule} is
configured in \texttt{AppModule} with a default budget of
\textbf{100 requests per 60-second window} per client, and a
\texttt{ThrottlerGuard} is registered as a global \texttt{APP\_GUARD}
so the limit covers every route without per-controller wiring. Two
routes deliberately diverge from the default:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Checkout} (\texttt{POST /payments/checkout}) is
        tightened to \textbf{10 requests per minute} with
        \texttt{@Throttle}, because each call reserves stock and opens a
        Stripe intent --- operations far more expensive than a read.
  \item \textbf{The Stripe webhook} opts out entirely with
        \texttt{@SkipThrottle}: its call rate is dictated by Stripe, not
        by a client, and throttling it could drop legitimate
        payment-confirmation events.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stale-Checkout Reconciliation}

Confirming orders by webhook introduces two failure modes that a naive
flow would leave unresolved: a customer who abandons checkout leaves an
\texttt{UNPAID} order holding reserved stock indefinitely, and a webhook
lost in transit leaves a genuinely paid order stuck as \texttt{UNPAID}.
A scheduled reconciliation sweep closes both gaps and makes the system
self-healing.

A small \texttt{PaymentCleanupService} runs every ten minutes via the
\texttt{@Cron} scheduler of \texttt{@nestjs/schedule} (enabled with
\texttt{ScheduleModule.forRoot()}) and delegates to
\texttt{PaymentService.reconcileStaleCheckouts}. That method collects
every order still \texttt{UNPAID} past a configurable staleness window
(\texttt{CHECKOUT\_STALE\_MINUTES}, default~30), groups them by their
\texttt{PaymentIntent}, retrieves each intent from Stripe, and acts on
its real status:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \texttt{succeeded} --- the success webhook was lost; the orders
        are marked \texttt{PAID}, recovering the missed event.
  \item \texttt{requires\_payment\_method} or \texttt{canceled} --- the
        intent is dead; the orders are cancelled and their stock
        released, and a not-yet-cancelled intent is cancelled on Stripe
        as best-effort cleanup.
  \item \texttt{processing}, \texttt{requires\_action} or
        \texttt{requires\_confirmation} --- the payment is still in
        flight and is left untouched for a later sweep.
\end{itemize}

Orders that never received an intent are simply restocked. Each intent
is reconciled inside its own \texttt{try}/\texttt{catch}, so a single
Stripe error cannot abort the whole sweep; and because the sweep reuses
the same idempotent \texttt{markPaid} / \texttt{markFailedAndRestock}
transitions as the live webhook, the two can race without corrupting
order state. This behaviour is covered by the
\texttt{reconcileStaleCheckouts} unit tests of Section~\ref{sec:tests}.

\section{Frontend (React + Vite)}

\subsection{Project Structure}

The frontend is a React~19 single-page application built with Vite.
Source is organised by responsibility under \texttt{src/}:
\texttt{pages/} holds route-level screens (further nested into
\texttt{dashboard/admin/} and \texttt{dashboard/seller/});
\texttt{components/} holds reusable presentational components and the
shadcn/ui primitives under \texttt{components/ui/}; \texttt{routes/}
holds the route table and the \texttt{RoleRoute} guard;
\texttt{hooks/} holds the data-fetching hooks that wrap TanStack
Query; \texttt{stores/} holds the Zustand stores; \texttt{api/} holds
the Axios client and typed request functions; and \texttt{lib/},
\texttt{constants/}, and \texttt{types/} hold shared utilities,
enumerations, and TypeScript types. The \texttt{@/} path alias maps to
\texttt{src/} in both the Vite config and \texttt{tsconfig}, so imports
read \texttt{@/components/ProductCard} regardless of nesting depth.

\subsection{Routing and Layouts}

Routing uses \texttt{react-router-dom}~7. The application is wrapped
in \texttt{main.tsx} by four providers, in order: a
\texttt{HelmetProvider} for per-page document titles, the TanStack
\texttt{QueryClientProvider}, a \texttt{BrowserRouter}, and the app's
own \texttt{useAuthStoreSync} hook that hydrates the auth store from
the active session on load.

The route table itself lives in \texttt{src/routes/index.tsx}. Public
routes --- home, store listing, store page, product listing, product
page, cart, checkout, login, register --- are declared directly.
Protected routes are nested inside a \texttt{RoleRoute} element that
takes an \texttt{allowedRoles} list and a \texttt{redirectTo} target:
the \texttt{/dashboard} and Craft editor routes are gated to
\texttt{ADMIN} and \texttt{SELLER}, while \texttt{/my-account} and the
seller-application flow are gated to \texttt{CUSTOMER}. A user whose
role is not in the allow-list is redirected rather than shown the
screen, mirroring the backend's \texttt{@Roles()} enforcement on the
API side so that authorisation is consistent end-to-end.

\subsection{State Management (TanStack Query + Zustand)}

The frontend deliberately separates \emph{server state} from
\emph{client state}, using a different tool for each.

\textbf{TanStack Query}~\cite{tanstack} owns all server state. Every read from the
API goes through a custom hook in \texttt{src/hooks/}
(\texttt{useProducts}, \texttt{useStorePage}, \texttt{useMyOrders},
\texttt{useFeatured}, \texttt{useAdminDashboard},
\texttt{useNotifications}, and others) that wraps
\texttt{useQuery}/\texttt{useMutation}. The shared
\texttt{QueryClient} (in \texttt{src/lib/queryClient.ts}) is
configured with a one-minute \texttt{staleTime} and a single retry,
which keeps the UI responsive (cached data is reused across
navigations) without hammering the API. Mutations invalidate the
relevant query keys so the cache stays coherent after writes.

\textbf{Zustand}~\cite{zustand} owns the small amount of genuine client state that
must persist across routes and reloads. The cart store
(\texttt{src/stores/cart.store.ts}) is the principal example: it holds
the customer's purchase intent, enforces the per-item stock cap on
add and update, and is wrapped in Zustand's \texttt{persist}
middleware so the cart survives a page refresh via
\texttt{localStorage}. The auth store holds the current user and role
for the \texttt{RoleRoute} guard. This division --- TanStack Query for
anything that lives on the server, Zustand for anything that is purely
the client's --- avoids the common pitfall of duplicating server data
into a global store and then having to keep the two in sync.

\section{AI Integration with n8n}

All AI features are implemented as n8n workflows triggered over
webhooks rather than as in-process calls from NestJS. This keeps the
LLM-provider credentials, prompt templates, and parsing logic outside
the application code, where they can be edited visually and re-run
against historical executions without redeploying the backend. The
backend's only responsibility is to fire the webhook with a compact
payload and act on the structured response.

\subsection{AI Product/Store Verification Pipeline}

Because a product's (or store's) images are uploaded \emph{after}
the entity itself is created, verification is not fired at creation
time --- doing so would screen a listing that has no images yet.
Instead, once the client has finished uploading the images it calls
\texttt{POST /\{type\}/:id/submit-verification}, and the
corresponding service fires the \textbf{cheebo-ai-verify} webhook
asynchronously (fire-and-forget, so the request never blocks on the
AI). The payload carries the entity \texttt{type} (\texttt{product}
or \texttt{store}), its \texttt{id}, \texttt{name},
\texttt{description}, and the list of image URLs. The workflow
downloads each image from the backend's \texttt{/uploads} route and
screens it --- together with the listing text --- with the Gemini
vision model, flagging the entity if \emph{any} image violates the
platform's content policy. It then calls back into the API ---
\texttt{PATCH /product/:id/ai-review} or the equivalent store route,
authenticated by the \texttt{x-api-key} header that the
\texttt{ApiKeyGuard} validates --- with an \texttt{approved} or
\texttt{flagged} decision and a reason.

The backend records the outcome on two distinct flags:
\texttt{aiReviewed} (set by this pipeline, with the reason stored in
\texttt{aiReviewNote}) and \texttt{adminReviewed} (set only when a
human administrator later overrides the decision). Separating the two
means the admin moderation queue can show \emph{why} the AI flagged a
listing and still let a human have the final say --- the override
path is exactly what the \texttt{verify()} and \texttt{aiReview()}
service tests in Section~\ref{sec:tests} pin down.

\subsection{Database Chat Assistant}

The database chat assistant is an administrator-only tool, surfaced as
a floating button (\texttt{AiChatFab}) on the admin dashboard. The
administrator asks operational questions in natural language ---
\textit{"how many orders shipped this week?"}, \textit{"which stores
are still pending verification?"} --- and receives an answer grounded
in live data. The frontend posts the question to the NestJS
\texttt{ChatModule}, which forwards it to the \textbf{cheebo-ai-chat}
n8n workflow. There an AI Agent translates the question into a safe,
read-only database query, executes it, and summarises the result set
in prose. Because the assistant is mounted only inside the
role-gated admin dashboard, it is unreachable by customers or
sellers --- a boundary verified both by the \texttt{RoleRoute} guard
on the frontend and the \texttt{AuthGuard}/\texttt{@Roles} pairing on
the backend.

\subsection{n8n Workflow --- AI Storefront Redesign}

The AI Storefront Redesign workflow allows a seller to regenerate their
entire store visual identity by providing a short natural-language prompt
from inside the Craft.js editor. The end-to-end interaction sequence
between seller, backend, n8n and the LLM provider was presented in the
design chapter (Figure~\ref{fig:seq-ai-redesign-design}); this section
describes its implementation.

The seller clicks the \textit{AI Redesign} button in the editor toolbar,
which opens a dialog prompting for a short description of the desired
aesthetic (e.g.\ \textit{"dark luxury jewellery brand with gold accents"}).
The React frontend calls \texttt{POST /store/:id/ai-redesign}, passing the
prompt alongside the store name and description for context.

On the backend, the NestJS Store Module forwards the request to the
\textbf{cheebo-store-redesign} n8n webhook.
The workflow consists of three nodes:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Build Prompt} --- a Code node that constructs a
        system-level instruction enforcing a strict JSON schema matching
        the \texttt{StoreCustomization} type. The schema covers every
        configurable section: theme colours and fonts, announcement bar,
        banner, header, product grid, sidebar, and footer.

  \item \textbf{AI Agent} --- an AI Agent node connected to the Groq
        \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} model. The agent receives the
        assembled system prompt and the seller's free-text request, then
        returns a JSON object conforming to the \texttt{StoreCustomization}
        schema.

  \item \textbf{Parse \& Sanitise} --- a Code node that parses the raw
        LLM output, validates all hex colour values with a regex, clamps
        numeric fields to valid ranges, and falls back to safe defaults for
        any field that is missing or malformed.
\end{enumerate}

The sanitised \texttt{StoreCustomization} object is returned to the NestJS
backend, which merges it with the store defaults and persists it through
the existing \texttt{updateCustomization} path. The frontend then calls
\texttt{hydrateCustomization()} on the response and applies the result
directly to the Craft.js editor, giving the seller an immediate live
preview of the AI-generated design without a page reload.

\subsection{Google Gemini / Groq Integration}

The workflows are model-agnostic by design --- the AI Agent node
abstracts the provider --- but in the deployed configuration two
providers are used, chosen per task:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Verification} (\textbf{cheebo-ai-verify}) uses a Google
        Gemini~\cite{gemini} model, whose vision capability is required to moderate the
        uploaded images against the listing text. The \textbf{database
        chat assistant} (\textbf{cheebo-ai-chat}) uses a Groq
        \texttt{openai/gpt-oss-20b} agent with a Gemini chat model
        available as an alternate within the same workflow. These tasks
        are classification- and reasoning-oriented and do not need a very
        large model.
  \item \textbf{Storefront redesign} (\textbf{cheebo-store-redesign})
        uses Groq's~\cite{groq} \texttt{llama-3.3-70b-versatile}. The larger model
        produces more coherent full-page design JSON, and Groq's
        inference speed keeps the seller's live-preview latency low.
\end{itemize}

Provider credentials are stored in n8n's encrypted credential store
(persisted to the \texttt{n8n\_data} volume), never in the
application's environment or source. Swapping a provider, or
A/B-testing two models, is a change inside n8n that requires no
backend redeployment.

\subsection{n8n Workflow Implementation}

Each workflow follows the same skeleton: a \textbf{Webhook} trigger
(secured with header authentication), an \textbf{AI Agent} bound to a
chat model, one or more \textbf{Code} nodes for prompt assembly and
output sanitisation, and a \textbf{Respond to Webhook} node that
returns the structured result to NestJS. The three workflows ---
\texttt{Products/Stores verification}, \texttt{Cheebo DB Chat}, and
\texttt{cheebo-store-redesign} --- are exported as JSON and committed
to the repository under \texttt{n8n-workflows/}, so the automation
layer is versioned alongside the application code and can be
re-imported into a fresh n8n instance. The full JSON exports are
reproduced in Appendix~\ref{app:n8n-workflows}.

The redesign workflow is the most elaborate of the three and was
detailed above; the verification and chat workflows are simpler
two-to-three-node graphs that the sanitisation Code node keeps
defensive against malformed LLM output --- the same robustness
principle applied throughout the backend.

\section{Tests and Validation}
\label{sec:tests}

Cheebo ships with an automated test suite that runs on every push to
\texttt{main} as a gate before the Docker images are built and
deployed. The suite follows a layered strategy: pure-logic unit tests
at the bottom, service-level unit tests with a mocked Prisma client in
the middle, and controller-level integration tests at the top that
boot a real NestJS application and exercise the full HTTP pipeline
(\texttt{ValidationPipe}, guards, decorators) through
\texttt{supertest}. The frontend uses Vitest with React Testing
Library~\cite{rtl} to cover Zustand stores and presentational components in a
jsdom environment.

The whole suite runs in under five seconds on commodity hardware,
which keeps the feedback loop short enough to be useful during
development and cheap enough to run on every CI build. No production
database is required: services are tested against in-memory mocks of
\texttt{PrismaService}, and HTTP-layer tests stub the guards that
transitively import the ESM-only \texttt{better-auth} package.

\subsection{Continuous Integration}

All tests run as gating jobs in the GitHub Actions workflow before
the Docker images are built. The pipeline includes two new jobs ---
\texttt{test-backend} (\texttt{pnpm test} = Jest) and
\texttt{test-frontend} (\texttt{pnpm test} = Vitest) --- and the
existing \texttt{build-backend}/\texttt{build-frontend} jobs declare
\texttt{needs: test-backend} and \texttt{needs: test-frontend}
respectively. A failing test therefore prevents any Docker image
from being pushed to Docker Hub, and by extension prevents
deployment to the DigitalOcean droplet. The mechanics of this gate
are detailed in Section~\ref{sec:cicd}.

\subsection{What is Not Covered}

The suite deliberately stops short of full end-to-end testing. There
are no Playwright or Cypress tests that drive a real browser against
the deployed application, and no integration tests against a real
MariaDB instance. These were considered out of scope for a six-month
PFE: a meaningful E2E suite requires fixtures, seeded data, and a
running stack, which would have consumed time better spent on the
core feature set. The current strategy --- tight unit coverage on
business rules plus thin HTTP-layer integration tests --- catches the
majority of regressions in the parts of the codebase where they
would do the most damage, at a fraction of the engineering cost.

\begin{keytakeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Cheebo ships 192 tests (171 backend, 21 frontend) running in
        a few seconds and gating every CI build.
  \item Backend tests use a three-tier pyramid: pure logic
        (\texttt{findViolation}, \texttt{tndToStripeUsdMinor},
        \texttt{ApiKeyGuard}), service-level
        unit tests with a mocked \texttt{PrismaService}, and
        controller-level integration tests with the real Nest
        pipeline.
  \item Frontend tests use Vitest + React Testing Library to cover
        the Zustand cart store, \texttt{ProductCard} sale-price
        rendering, and the Stripe checkout step.
  \item Test execution is wired into GitHub Actions: a failing test
        blocks the Docker image push and deployment, providing a
        hard regression gate without manual review.
\end{itemize}
\end{keytakeaways}

\section{Containerisation with Docker}

\subsection{Dockerfiles (Frontend and Backend)}

Both the backend and the frontend ship as multi-stage
Docker~\cite{docker} images,
which keeps the final runtime image small by discarding build-time
dependencies. Both Dockerfiles are reproduced in
Appendix~\ref{app:dockerfiles}.

The \textbf{backend} image uses a two-stage
\texttt{node:20-alpine} build. The \emph{builder}
stage enables \texttt{pnpm} through Corepack, installs dependencies
from the frozen lockfile, runs \texttt{prisma generate}, and compiles
the TypeScript to \texttt{dist/}. The \emph{production} stage copies
only the compiled output, the generated Prisma client, the
\texttt{node\_modules}, and the schema --- not the source tree. The
container's start command first runs \texttt{prisma migrate deploy}
(applying any pending migrations against the live database) and then
launches the compiled server, so a fresh deployment self-migrates with
no manual step.

The \textbf{frontend} image is also two-stage: a
\texttt{node:20-alpine} builder runs \texttt{pnpm run build} to emit
the static Vite bundle, and the production stage serves it from
\texttt{nginx:alpine}. Because the API URL is only known at deploy
time (it depends on the domain), the build bakes in a placeholder
token and an \texttt{entrypoint.sh} script rewrites it to the real
\texttt{VITE\_API\_URL} on container start --- a single image that can
target any environment without rebuilding. The bundled Nginx config
serves the SPA with an \texttt{index.html} fallback, reverse-proxies
\texttt{/api/} to the backend, and gives the
\texttt{/notification/stream} SSE endpoint special handling
(buffering and gzip disabled, long read timeout) so live
notifications are not held back by the proxy.

\subsection{Docker Compose Configuration}

The full stack is orchestrated by a single \texttt{docker-compose.yml}
(Appendix~\ref{app:docker-compose}) declaring five services and five
named volumes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{db} --- \texttt{mariadb:11} with a health-check
        (\texttt{mariadb-admin ping}) so dependants can wait until the
        database is genuinely ready, persisted to the \texttt{db\_data}
        volume. A dedicated least-privilege application user
        (\texttt{DB\_APP\_USER}) is created on first boot and used by the
        backend, so the application never connects as \texttt{root}.
  \item \textbf{backend} --- the NestJS image, which runs with
        \texttt{NODE\_ENV=production} (which, among other things, disables
        Swagger), \texttt{depends\_on} the database \emph{being healthy}, reads
        all configuration from environment variables, and mounts the
        \texttt{uploads} volume for user-uploaded media.
  \item \textbf{frontend} --- the Nginx image serving the React
        bundle; it exposes no host port and is reached only through
        the Caddy proxy.
  \item \textbf{caddy} --- the public entrypoint, the only service
        that publishes host ports (\texttt{80} and \texttt{443}). It
        terminates HTTPS and reverse-proxies to the frontend
        (Section~\ref{sec:tls-caddy}).
  \item \textbf{n8n} --- the automation engine, publishing
        \texttt{5678} for inbound webhooks and persisting workflows and
        credentials to \texttt{n8n\_data}.
\end{itemize}

Every service is set to \texttt{restart: always}, so the stack
recovers automatically after a host reboot. Configuration is injected
through a \texttt{.env} file written by the deploy job
(Section~\ref{sec:cicd}), keeping secrets out of the committed
Compose file.

\subsection{TLS Termination with Caddy}
\label{sec:tls-caddy}

Public traffic does not reach the application containers directly: it
is fronted by \textbf{Caddy}~\cite{caddy}, a modern reverse proxy whose defining
feature is \emph{automatic HTTPS}. On first start, Caddy reads the
domain from its environment, requests a TLS certificate from Let's
Encrypt over the ACME protocol (the HTTP-01 / TLS-ALPN-01 challenge,
served on the ports it already owns), installs it, and renews it
before expiry --- with no certbot cron job, no manual key handling,
and no application involvement.

The configuration is a short, env-driven \texttt{Caddyfile}
(Appendix~\ref{app:docker-compose}) with two site blocks: the
\texttt{www} subdomain issues a permanent redirect to the apex so
that the platform has a single canonical HTTPS origin, and the apex
block reverse-proxies the decrypted traffic to the internal Nginx
frontend on \texttt{frontend:80}. Keeping one canonical origin matters
beyond aesthetics: it must match the backend's \texttt{BETTER\_AUTH\_URL}
and \texttt{trustedOrigins} so that session cookies --- now issued with
the \texttt{Secure} attribute because the origin is \texttt{https} ---
are accepted consistently. The certificate and ACME account state are
persisted to the \texttt{caddy\_data} volume, so redeployments reuse
the existing certificate and never risk hitting Let's Encrypt's
issuance rate limits.

Introducing Caddy required no change to the application code. Because
Better-Auth derives the \texttt{Secure} cookie flag from its
\texttt{baseURL}, the cutover to HTTPS was achieved simply by setting
\texttt{APP\_URL} to the \texttt{https} origin in the deploy job; this
value cascades to \texttt{BETTER\_AUTH\_URL}, \texttt{FRONTEND\_URL}
(the CORS and trusted origin), and the frontend's \texttt{VITE\_API\_URL}.

\subsection{Multi-Service Networking}

All services communicate over a private Docker bridge network,
meaning the frontend, backend, database, and n8n are never directly
exposed to the public internet. Only the Caddy proxy publishes ports
(80 and 443). Figure~\ref{fig:docker-network} shows the network
topology: Caddy terminates HTTPS and forwards to the internal Nginx
frontend, which in turn proxies all API paths to the backend service
over the internal network.

\section{Infrastructure as Code with Terraform}

\subsection{DigitalOcean Provider}

The entire production environment is described declaratively with
Terraform~\cite{terraform}, using the official \texttt{digitalocean/digitalocean}
provider~\cite{digitalocean} (\texttt{\textasciitilde> 2.0}). A single \texttt{main.tf}
holds the provider block --- authenticated by a DigitalOcean API
token passed as the \texttt{do\_token} variable --- and all resource
declarations. Describing the infrastructure as code means the droplet,
network, and DNS can be recreated identically from scratch with one
\texttt{terraform apply}, and any change is reviewable as a diff
rather than a series of clicks in a web console.

\subsection{VPC, Droplet and Firewall Resources}

Three resources define the compute environment:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{\texttt{digitalocean\_vpc}} --- a private network
        (\texttt{192.168.1.0/24}) in the target region, isolating the
        droplet's private interface.
  \item \textbf{\texttt{digitalocean\_droplet}} --- an
        \texttt{ubuntu-24-04-x64} droplet of the configured size,
        attached to the VPC, with the operator's SSH key injected for
        access.
  \item \textbf{Firewall (UFW)} --- configured through the droplet's
        \texttt{user\_data} cloud-init script rather than a separate
        resource.
\end{itemize}

The \texttt{user\_data} script is the key to a zero-touch first boot:
on creation, the droplet installs Docker Engine and the Compose
plugin from Docker's official APT repository, enables the service,
and then configures UFW to \emph{deny all inbound by default} while
allowing only SSH (22), HTTP (80), HTTPS (443), and the n8n webhook
port (5678). Because this runs as part of provisioning, a freshly
created droplet is ready to receive the Compose stack without any
manual server setup --- the CI/CD deploy job only has to copy the
Compose file and bring the stack up.

\subsection{DNS Management with DigitalOcean}

Rather than managing DNS records at the domain registrar (name.com) through a
separate API, the project delegates DNS entirely to DigitalOcean using the same
Terraform provider that manages the droplet.  Two resources are declared in
\texttt{main.tf}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{digitalocean\_domain} --- creates the DNS zone for
        \texttt{nadimjebali.engineer} on DigitalOcean's authoritative nameservers
        and automatically adds the apex (\texttt{@}) A record pointing to the
        droplet's IPv4 address.
  \item \texttt{digitalocean\_record} --- adds a second A record for the
        \texttt{www} subdomain, also pointing to the droplet.
\end{itemize}

Both records reference \texttt{digitalocean\_droplet.cheebo.ipv4\_address}
directly.  This means that if the droplet is ever replaced (e.g.\ after a
destructive Terraform change), Terraform updates both DNS records in the same
\texttt{apply} run that creates the new droplet, with no manual intervention
required.

The domain's nameservers are delegated to DigitalOcean
(\texttt{ns1--ns3.digitalocean.com}) from the name.com registrar panel, which
is a one-time configuration step.  After that, A-record updates propagate in
under five minutes thanks to a 300-second TTL.

CI/CD run reads from and writes to a single shared state file,

\section{CI/CD Pipeline with GitHub Actions}
\label{sec:cicd}

\subsection{Workflow Overview}

The CI/CD pipeline is defined as a single GitHub
Actions~\cite{gha} workflow
file that runs on every push to the \texttt{main} branch.
Figure~\ref{fig:cicd} presents the topology:
two test jobs --- \textit{test-backend} (Jest) and
\textit{test-frontend} (Vitest) --- run in parallel and gate the
corresponding image-build jobs, \textit{build-backend} and
\textit{build-frontend}, via the \texttt{needs:} dependency.
A failing test therefore short-circuits the entire pipeline before
any Docker image is pushed to Docker Hub. The
\textit{provision} job (Terraform) runs in parallel and applies the
infrastructure plan against DigitalOcean. The \textit{deploy} job
runs last and SSH-deploys the composed stack to the droplet once
the two builds and the provisioning step all complete. The full
test catalogue gating this pipeline is presented in
Section~\ref{sec:tests}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \imagePlaceholder[7cm]{CI/CD pipeline diagram (build $\rightarrow$ push $\rightarrow$ deploy)}
  \caption{GitHub Actions CI/CD pipeline}
  \label{fig:cicd}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Build Stage (Docker Build and Push to Docker Hub)}

The \texttt{build-backend} and \texttt{build-frontend} jobs each run
only after their respective test job passes. Both use Docker Buildx
with the GitHub Actions cache backend (\texttt{cache-from}/
\texttt{cache-to} with \texttt{type=gha}), so unchanged layers ---
notably the \texttt{pnpm install} layer --- are reused across runs,
cutting build times substantially. Each job logs in to Docker Hub
with credentials drawn from repository secrets, builds the
multi-stage image from its package directory, and pushes it to the
\texttt{sirvinny/cheebo-backend} and \texttt{sirvinny/cheebo-frontend}
repositories under two tags: \texttt{:latest} and an immutable
commit-SHA tag. The deploy step pins the Compose stack to the SHA tag
(via an \texttt{IMAGE\_TAG} variable), so every deployment runs an exact,
identifiable build and a rollback is simply a redeploy of an earlier
commit's tag.

\subsection{Deployment Stage (SSH and Docker Compose)}

The \texttt{deploy} job runs last, gated on \texttt{needs:
[provision, build-backend, build-frontend]} so it only fires once the
infrastructure exists and both images are published. It performs two
actions over SSH to the droplet (whose IP comes from the
\texttt{provision} job's Terraform output):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Copy} the \texttt{docker-compose.yml}, the
        \texttt{Caddyfile}, and the version-controlled
        \texttt{n8n-workflows/} and \texttt{n8n-credentials/}
        template directories to \texttt{/app} on the droplet via
        \texttt{scp}.
  \item \textbf{Run} a remote script that waits for cloud-init to
        finish, writes the \texttt{.env} file from the workflow's
        environment, then executes \texttt{docker compose pull}
        followed by \texttt{docker compose up -d
        --force-recreate}.
\end{enumerate}

Because the images are pulled fresh and the backend self-applies
migrations on start, a push to \texttt{main} results in a fully
updated, migrated production stack with no manual intervention ---
the deployment is idempotent and repeatable.

\subsection{Automated n8n Provisioning}

The same deploy step also provisions the n8n instance, so the AI
features work on a freshly created droplet with no manual clicking in
the n8n editor. The workflow definitions and credential
\emph{templates} are version-controlled under
\texttt{n8n-workflows/} and \texttt{n8n-credentials/}; the remote
script renders the templates with \texttt{envsubst}, injecting the
provider API keys and the database/webhook secrets from the
environment (an explicit variable allow-list leaves n8n's own
\texttt{\$json} expressions untouched). A dedicated one-shot
\texttt{n8n-init} container then imports the credentials and
workflows and activates them, running to completion \emph{before} the
main \texttt{n8n} service starts (\texttt{depends\_on} with
\texttt{service\_completed\_successfully}), which avoids two processes
writing n8n's database simultaneously. Because the imports upsert by
identifier, editing a workflow in the repository and redeploying
updates the live instance --- a GitOps-style flow that keeps the
entire AI layer reproducible.

\subsection{Environment Secrets Management}

No secret is ever committed to the repository. Sensitive values
(database root password, the least-privilege \texttt{DB\_APP\_PASSWORD},
\texttt{BETTER\_AUTH\_SECRET}, admin password,
SMTP password, n8n webhook secret and encryption key, the Gemini and
Groq API keys, DigitalOcean and Spaces tokens, the SSH private key)
are stored as GitHub Actions
\textbf{secrets}; non-sensitive configuration (domain, ports, feature
limits, SMTP host, the \texttt{DB\_APP\_USER} name) is stored as GitHub
Actions \textbf{variables}.
The deploy job interpolates both groups into the \texttt{.env} file it
writes on the droplet at deploy time. Terraform secrets reach the
\texttt{provision} job the same way, including the Spaces credentials
used to read and write the remote state backend. This keeps the
single source of truth for every credential inside GitHub's encrypted
store, with the droplet's \texttt{.env} as the only materialised copy
--- created at deploy time and never leaving the host.

\subsection{n8n Deployment Alongside the Application}

n8n runs as a fifth container in the same Compose stack rather than
as an external SaaS dependency. This co-location is deliberate: the
backend reaches the workflows over the private bridge network at
\texttt{http://n8n:5678/webhook/\dots}, so verification and chat
calls never leave the host, and the only n8n port exposed publicly
(5678) is for \emph{inbound} webhook delivery and the editor UI.
Workflow definitions and provider credentials are encrypted with
\texttt{N8N\_ENCRYPTION\_KEY} and persisted to the \texttt{n8n\_data}
volume, so they survive container re-creation across deployments.

Hosting the automation engine on the same droplet keeps the entire
platform --- application, database, and AI orchestration --- within a
single, reproducible Compose deployment and a single monthly hosting
cost, which suits the scale and budget of a graduation project. The
trade-off is that n8n shares the droplet's 2~vCPU/2~GB with the rest
of the stack; for a production launch at scale it would be moved to
its own instance, but at current load the shared host is comfortable.

\begin{keytakeaways}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item Both applications ship as multi-stage Docker images; the
        five-service Compose stack exposes only the Caddy proxy,
        which terminates HTTPS with automatically renewed
        Let's Encrypt certificates.
  \item The entire production environment --- VPC, droplet, firewall
        and DNS --- is declared in Terraform with remote state in
        DigitalOcean Spaces; the DNS records follow the droplet IP
        automatically, so a full re-provision converges in under ten
        minutes end-to-end.
  \item The GitHub Actions pipeline gates every deployment on the
        test suite, pins deployments to immutable commit-SHA image
        tags, and provisions the n8n workflows and credentials on
        the droplet automatically.
  \item No secret is ever committed: credentials live in GitHub's
        encrypted store and materialise only as the droplet's
        \texttt{.env} file at deploy time.
\end{itemize}
\end{keytakeaways}

\section*{Chapter Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Chapter Conclusion}

This chapter presented the infrastructure that carries Cheebo in
production. The five services were containerised with multi-stage
Docker builds and orchestrated by a single Compose file, fronted by
a Caddy proxy that provides automatic HTTPS. Terraform describes the
entire DigitalOcean environment --- droplet, VPC, firewall rules and
DNS --- as version-controlled code with shared remote state, and the
GitHub Actions pipeline chains testing, image builds, provisioning
and SSH deployment into a single push-to-deploy workflow, including
the automated provisioning of the n8n AI layer. The platform is
thereby reproducible from an empty cloud account to a running,
TLS-secured marketplace with no manual step. The general conclusion
that follows takes stock of the project as a whole.

\chapter*{General Conclusion}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Conclusion}
\markboth{General Conclusion}{}

This project set out to design and build Cheebo, a multi-vendor
marketplace for the Tunisian market with AI-assisted moderation, as a
final-year project at TEK-UP in partnership with NeuralBey. The
preceding chapters traced the work from market context and
requirements, through system design, to implementation, testing, and
deployment. This conclusion summarises what was achieved, measures it
against the original plan, reflects on the challenges met along the
way, states the limitations that remain, and outlines the directions
in which the platform could grow.

\section*{Summary of Achievements}

The project delivered a complete, deployed, three-actor marketplace.
Customers can browse stores and products, search the catalogue, build
a multi-store cart, and place orders; sellers can apply for an
account, open a store, manage a product catalogue, run store-wide
sales, and redesign their storefront visually through a Craft.js
editor; administrators can moderate stores and products, manage users,
curate featured content, and query the platform in natural language.
On top of the functional surface, three cross-cutting capabilities set
the project apart from a textbook CRUD application:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{AI integration} --- product and store verification,
        a database chat assistant, and AI storefront redesign, all
        implemented as versioned n8n workflows behind webhooks.
  \item \textbf{A real test suite} --- 192 automated tests across
        backend and frontend, gating every CI build.
  \item \textbf{A full DevOps pipeline} --- containerised services,
        infrastructure as code with Terraform, and a GitHub Actions
        pipeline that tests, builds, provisions, and deploys to a live
        domain with no manual step.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Implemented Features vs Initially Planned Features}

The large majority of the Product Backlog defined in
Chapter~\ref{chap:requirements} was implemented. All \emph{Must-have}
and \emph{Should-have} stories --- authentication, store and product
management, the order system, the admin dashboard, AI verification,
and featured-content curation --- are present in the deployed
platform. Two \emph{Could-have} items added mid-project, the
store-wide sale feature and AI storefront redesign, were also
completed.

The principal gap against the original vision is \textbf{online
payment}: orders are currently placed on a cash-on-delivery basis
rather than through an integrated payment gateway. This was a
conscious scoping decision --- integrating and testing a payment
provider reliably was judged a poor use of the remaining time
relative to strengthening the core marketplace and its DevOps and
test foundations. It is the first item on the future-work list below.

\section*{Challenges Encountered and Solutions Provided}

Several non-trivial problems were solved during development:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=3pt]
  \item \textbf{Structured output from an LLM.} The AI storefront
        redesign needs the model to return JSON conforming exactly to
        the \texttt{StoreCustomization} schema. Raw model output is
        not reliably valid, so a sanitisation step parses the
        response, validates every colour with a regex, clamps numeric
        fields, and falls back to safe defaults --- turning a
        best-effort model into a dependable component.
  \item \textbf{Premium gating without data loss on downgrade.} Free
        accounts must be prevented from editing premium customization
        fields, yet a seller whose subscription lapses must not lose
        their existing design. The diff-based \texttt{findViolation}
        check allows a field to remain at its current value while
        blocking changes --- behaviour pinned by dedicated unit tests.
  \item \textbf{Zero-touch, repeatable deployment.} Coordinating
        Terraform provisioning, image builds, database migrations, and
        SSH deployment into one idempotent pipeline required the
        droplet to self-configure via cloud-init and the backend to
        self-migrate on start, so a push to \texttt{main} converges to
        a fully updated production stack unattended.
  \item \textbf{Testing a codebase wired to ESM-only and native
        dependencies.} Better-Auth (ESM-only) and the Prisma~7 client
        broke the default Jest setup; module-name mappers and
        module-level guard stubs were introduced so the suite runs
        without a live database.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Current Implementation Limitations}

The platform is a complete prototype, not yet a production launch.
The main limitations are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{No online payment} --- orders are cash-on-delivery
        only.
  \item \textbf{Single-droplet, shared-host deployment} --- the
        application, database, and n8n share one 2~vCPU/2~GB droplet,
        which suits the project's scale but is not horizontally
        scalable as-is.
  \item \textbf{No end-to-end test layer} --- the test suite covers
        units and HTTP-layer integration, but not full
        browser-driven flows against the deployed stack
        (Section~\ref{sec:tests}).
\end{itemize}

\section*{Future Improvements}

Natural next steps, roughly in order of value:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Online payment} --- integrate a Tunisian payment
        gateway such as Konnect to support card and e-wallet
        checkout alongside cash-on-delivery.
  \item \textbf{Mobile application} --- a React Native client reusing
        the existing REST API and authentication.
  \item \textbf{Self-hosted local AI} --- run the moderation and chat
        models locally (e.g.\ via LM Studio / Ollama) to remove the
        dependency on external LLM providers and their per-call cost.
  \item \textbf{A dedicated recommendation engine} --- evolve the
        current tag-overlap recommendations into a collaborative- or
        content-based model trained on order history.
  \item \textbf{Advanced analytics} --- richer seller and
        platform dashboards (cohorts, conversion funnels, revenue
        forecasting) building on the existing analytics service.
  \item \textbf{Production hardening} --- an end-to-end test
        suite and a path to multi-host scaling (the application,
        database, and n8n currently share a single droplet).
\end{itemize}
